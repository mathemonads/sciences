\chapter{Mathematics}\label{app:math}

Notation and terminology are reviewed in Sections \ref{sec:notation}--\ref{sec:terminology}, respectively.

\section{Fields of real and complex numbers}\label{sec:notation}

Denote the totally ordered field of real numbers by
\begin{align*}
	\left\langle \R, \R \times \R \xrightarrow{+} \R, 0, \R \times \R \xrightarrow{\cdot} \R, 1, \leq \right\rangle
\end{align*}
and the field of complex numbers by
\begin{align*}
	\left\langle \C, \C \times \C \xrightarrow{+} \C, 0, \C \times \C \xrightarrow{\cdot} \C, 1, \C \xrightarrow{*} \C \right\rangle
\end{align*}
with involution $\bullet^*$ given by complex conjugation.

\section{Terminology}\label{sec:terminology}

A \emph{curve} in a topological space $X$ is a continuous map from an interval in $\R$ to $X$.
A curve in a topological module is called \emph{nonvanishing} if it is nowhere equal to the identity of addition.
For any curve $\vr(t)$ in a vector space with norm $\left\|\bullet\right\|$, the \emph{radius} of $\vr$ is the function $r$ defined pointwise by $r(t) = \left\|\vr(t)\right\|$.

Consider a vector space with inner product $\braket{\bullet, \star}$ and norm $\left\|\bullet\right\|$ induced by the inner product.
Define the \emph{square} of a vector $\vr$ to be its inner product $\braket{\vr, \vr}$ with itself and denote it by $\vr^2$.
Define the square of a curve $\vr$ to be the scalar function $\vr^2 = \braket{\vr, \vr}$ defined pointwise by $\vr^2(t) = r^2(t)$.
